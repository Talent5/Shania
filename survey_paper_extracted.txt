Shania K T Ruwende

Abstract
Acute Decompensated Heart Failure (ADHF) is a major driver of hospital admissions and early readmissions, imposing clinical and economic burdens. This survey connects a focused review of past research and a clear methods plan to the capstone project, “Predicting Hospital Readmission in Acute Decompensated Heart Failure (ADHF) Patients Using Machine Learning.” Using Mpofu et al. as the main reference, the survey looks at what causes ADHF readmissions, what data sources can be used (like MIMIC-III and eICU), how to prepare the data, how to fix class imbalance (such as using SMOTE), and which machine-learning models to test (logistic regression, Random Forest, XGBoost). It also covers Explainable AI tools like SHAP to help doctors understand model results.The survey links all of this to the project goalspredicting 30-day readmissions, selecting an ADHFspecific patient group, adding explainability, and comparing results with the LACE score. It then suggests a step-by-step pipeline, evaluation plan, and deployment ideas for building a working prototype that supports Education 5.0 and benefits local healthcare settings.
Introduction
Acute Decompensated Heart Failure (ADHF) is one of the main reasons people are admitted to the hospital and return soon after. Many hospitals report that more than 20% of ADHF patients come back within 30 days, showing that current methods for judging discharge risk and supporting patients after discharge are not enough. Common tools like the LACE score are easy to use, but they often miss the complex patterns and episode-specific details seen in ADHF cases.
The capstone project plans to create an ADHF-focused, explainable machine-learning system that predicts 30-day readmission, highlights useful clinical risk factors, and performs better than existing scores. This survey reviews the research needed to support and guide that work, with a focus on reproducibility, transparency, and real-world clinical use.
Acute Decompensated Heart Failure (ADHF) is a major cause of hospital admissions, and many patients return soon after discharge—often within 30 days. This high readmission rate puts pressure on hospitals and affects patient wellbeing.
Traditional tools like the LACE score cannot capture the full complexity of ADHF because they ignore important factors such as congestion levels, frailty, kidney function, and discharge stability. Machine learning can help by finding hidden patterns in patient data, but many existing models still have gaps. Some are not specific to ADHF, others lack key clinical features, and many struggle with imbalanced datasets where readmissions are rare.
Another challenge is explainability. Doctors need to understand why a model predicts a high risk so they can make informed decisions. Unfortunately, many ML models do not provide clear explanations.The base study by Mpofu et al. [23] shows the potential of ML in local healthcare but focuses on diabetic patients and uses basic techniques that are not sufficient for ADHF.
Therefore, there is a need for a model that:
Focuses specifically on ADHF patients
Uses richer clinical data
Handles imbalanced datasets properly
Provides clear explanations using tools like SHAP
Such a model can support better discharge decisions and help reduce preventable readmissions.
 Literature Review
This section reviews research on hospital readmission prediction, machine learning (ML), explainable AI (XAI), and factors that affect outcomes in heartfailure patients. The aim is to show what is already known, what works well, what still needs improvement, and how these findings relate to the base paper by Mpofu et al. [23]. The language here has been simplified to make the ideas easy to understand.
Many studies show that machine learning can predict hospital readmissions better than traditional methods. For example, ML models such as Random Forest, XGBoost, and Gradient Boosting have been used to analyse hospital data and have produced strong performance results [6], [8]. These models work well because they can learn patterns from large and complex datasets.
Davis et al. [10] found that ML models perform even better when they use engineered featuressuch as summary statistics and combined risk scoresrather than raw clinical measurements. Sabouri et al. [17] also showed that using several types of patient data , that is vitals, demographics, medical history, and medications, improves prediction accuracy.
Some studies focus on specific diseases. For example, Alnazari et al. [9] created a model for kidneytransplant patients, and Verma and Lin [19] used physical activity data to predict readmission in COPD patients. These diseasespecific models prove that using the right type of clinical features is important.
However, very few studies focus specifically on Acute Decompensated Heart Failure (ADHF).Most research mixes general heartfailure cases, even though ADHF has its own unique risks. This creates a clear gap that this project is addressing.
Explainable AI (XAI) in Healthcare
Modern Machine Learning models are often complex, so doctors need explanations to understand how predictions are made. Explainable AI tools help with this. SHAP and LIME are two popular explanation methods discussed in several papers [2], [3]. These techniques highlight which features contributed the most to a prediction.
Hossain et al. [11] point out that while XAI tools are helpful, they still have weaknesses such as inconsistency and difficulty handling complex models. Band et al. [5] also explain that explanations must be reliable and easy for clinicians to understand.
Several studies show practical benefits of XAI. For example, Mohanty et al. [18] used SHAP to help doctors understand frailtyrelated readmission predictions. Magboo and Magboo [12] demonstrated that SHAP provides clearer feature importance than older techniques.
Because ADHF cases often involve urgent decisionmaking, using SHAP in this project improves trust and transparency ,something missing in the base project by Mpofu et al. [23].
In most hospital datasets, fewer patients are readmitted compared to those who are not. This imbalance can cause ML models to perform poorly on the minority class (the readmitted group). Several papers offer different solutions.
Zhao et al. [1] introduced a mixed sampling method that combines oversampling and undersampling to improve balance. Basgall et al. [4] found that SMOTE-based techniques significantly improve prediction of minority cases when used with ML models. Felix and Lee [29] also reviewed many imbalance-handling techniques and concluded that preprocessing often works better than weighting the model.
Susan and Kumar [30] warn that oversampling must be done carefully because it can create unrealistic synthetic samples if applied incorrectly.
This project’s plan to use SMOTE and stratified sampling aligns well with these recommended strategies. In contrast, Mpofu et al. [23] did not apply advanced imbalance-handling techniques, which limits the accuracy of their model.
 Clinical Factors Affecting ADHF Readmission
Research shows that ADHF readmission is influenced by many factors. Iwata et al. [7] found that patients with lower functional ability have a much higher chance of being readmitted. Sudharshan et al. [31] showed that biomarkers such as BNP and creatinine are strong predictors.
Clinical stability at discharge is also important. Akkineni et al. [26] observed that patients who remain unstable when leaving the hospital tend to return sooner. Some treatments, such as ultrafiltration, may even increase readmission risk if not used properly, as shown by Siddiqui et al. [25].Studies also show that frailty and comorbidities, including mentalhealth conditions, increase readmission rates. Okoye et al. [20] found that frailtyrelated clusters of patients are more vulnerable to poor outcomes. Chopera et al. [27] reported that depression and anxiety raise readmission rates among diabetic patients.
These clinical findings highlight a major gap in Mpofu et al. [23]: they did not include key medical indicators that are important for heartfailure cases, such as biomarkers, frailty measures, or congestion status.
 Mpofu et al. (2024) Base Paper Critical Evaluation
Mpofu et al. [23] created a model to predict readmission in diabetic patients using several ML algorithms. Their work provides a good starting point for understanding ML-based hospital prediction in Zimbabwe. Strengths of their study include:
Use of structured clinical data
Testing several ML models to compare performance
Relevance to local healthcare needs
However, the study has several limitations:
No XAI tools were used, so predictions are not interpretable
Only basic preprocessing was applied, with no advanced imbalance-handling
Important clinical features were missing
Evaluation metrics were limited to accuracy instead of metrics like AUC or recall
No analysis of how patient conditions change over time
These gaps show why an improved, ADHFfocused model is necessary. This project addresses these limitations by adding richer clinical features, advanced preprocessing, and SHAP explanations.
Critical Analysis and Synthesis
Machine learning improves prediction accuracy: Studies such as [6], [8], [10], and [17] clearly demonstrate that ML models outperform traditional clinical scoring systems. This confirms that ML is suitable for predicting readmission in complex conditions like ADHF.
Importance of using diverse clinical features: Research consistently shows that predictions improve when models use multiple feature typesvitals, biomarkers, comorbidities, medication data, and functional scores. This supports this project’s decision to use richer clinical data.
Explainable AI increases trust: Tools like SHAP and LIME, used in studies [2], [3], [5], [11], [12], help clinicians understand predictions. Since ADHF decisions are high-risk, explainability is essential. This project’s use of SHAP aligns strongly with these findings.
Handling imbalanced data improves model fairness: Studies such as [1], [4], [29], and [30] show that imbalance-handling methods like SMOTE make models more accurate and less biased. This project’s methodology includes this, which strengthens model performance.
Weaknesses and Gaps in Existing Literature
Despite strong progress, several gaps remain:
Limited ADHF-specific research: Many studies focus on general heart-failure patients rather than ADHF. This reduces accuracy for short-term readmission, which is strongly influenced by acute clinical instability.
Lack of essential medical features: Some models do not include biomarkers, congestion levels, functional scores, or frailty indicesfactors shown to be strong predictors in studies [7], [20], [25], and [31].
Weak explainability integration: Although XAI is gaining attention, many ML models for readmission prediction still do not use SHAP or LIME. This creates a barrier to real-world adoption.
Evaluation metrics are often limited: Some studies focus heavily on accuracy, even though metrics like recall, precision, and AUC give a better picture of clinical usefulness.
The base paper by Mpofu et al. [23] provides a helpful foundation for understanding how machine learning can be used to predict hospital readmissions in Zimbabwe. However, it has several limitations when compared to the needs of ADHF readmission prediction. First, the study focuses on diabetic patients rather than heart-failure patients. This means it does not include important cardiovascular variables that strongly influence readmission risk. Second, it uses basic preprocessing methods without addressing class imbalance, which can reduce accuracy. Third, the study does not include explainable AI methods, making it difficult for healthcare workers to understand how predictions are made. Finally, the evaluation relies mostly on accuracy, which does not fully represent clinical usefulness.
This project improves on these weaknesses in several ways:
It focuses specifically on ADHF patients, who have unique short-term clinical risks.
It includes richer clinical data such as biomarkers, vitals, congestion indicators, and functional scores.
It uses SMOTE and stratified sampling to address data imbalance.
It applies SHAP for clear and transparent explanations.
It evaluates the model using clinically meaningful metrics like AUC, recall, and precision.
These improvements make your model more suitable for real hospital settings and help address the gaps left by previous studies.
Proposed Future Work
Future work should aim to further improve prediction accuracy and clinical usefulness. One important direction is to include more detailed clinical data, such as longitudinal measurements that show how a patient’s condition changes over time. Another possible improvement is to integrate social and behavioural data, such as medication adherence or lifestyle factors, which have been shown to affect readmission risk.
Future research could also explore combining machine-learning models with deep-learning techniques for better feature extraction. However, these models must still be explainable so clinicians can understand their decisions. Another area worth exploring is real-time prediction systems that update risk scores as new patient data becomes available.
Lastly, future models should aim to be tested and validated on local hospital datasets in Zimbabwe. This would ensure the system is better adapted to local patient profiles, clinical workflows, and resource constraints.
Conclusion
This survey examined machine learning and explainable AI approaches for predicting hospital readmission, with a focus on ADHF. The review showed that machine-learning models usually outperform traditional tools, especially when they use multiple types of clinical data. Explainable AI tools like SHAP are important for building trust and improving clinical decision-making.
The base study by Mpofu et al. [23] provides a useful starting point but lacks key features needed for accurate ADHF prediction. Your project addresses these gaps by using ADHF-specific data, advanced preprocessing, and strong explainability methods.
Overall, the literature confirms that there is a clear need for a more accurate, transparent, and clinically useful model to predict ADHF readmissions. Your project contributes toward meeting this need and sets a foundation for more advanced research and real-world applications.
